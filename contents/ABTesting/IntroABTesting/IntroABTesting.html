Intro to A/B testing

A/B testing

  • randomized experiment with two variants, A and B
  • gives a lot of broad quantitative data but not very deep qualitative data
  • control & variation group → randomly assigned
  • it is how data analysts determine whether one change causes another
  • goal: scientifically determine if there is causality among variables
  • setting up and interperting A/B tests is a key skill for data analysts
Specifically for tech-apps
  • general methodology use online to test new features
  • tell if you're reaching the peak of the mountain but no what mountain you should be on
  • goal: scientifically determine how to optimize a web site or a mobile apps
  • Optipedia: A/B Testing
How it works
  • modify a product to create a second version
  • half of users is shown the original version & half the modified version
  • their engagement with each experience is measured and collected
  • determine whether the change had a positive, negative or no effect on behaviour
A/B testing process
1. Collect data
  • analytics provide insight into identify where you can optimize
  • begin with high traffic areas
  • look for pages with low conversion rates or high drop-off rates
2. Identify goals
  • metrics you are using to determine the variation success
  • from clicking a button to product purchases and e-mail signups
  • what you desire to increase or change
3. Generate hypothesis
  • why you think variation will be better than the current version?
  • prioritize them by expected impact & difficulty of implementation
4. Create variations
  • make the desired changes to an element
  • QA experiment to make sure it works as expected
5. Run experiment
  • visitors will be randomly assigned to control or variation group
  • interaction with each experience is measured
6. Analyse results
  • show the difference between how the two versions performed
  • show if there is a statistically significant difference
Use it
when where
  • testing changes in the experience
  • short term return data
  • want to know if you are reaching the peak of the mountain
  • new ranking algorithm in a recommender system
  • change backend: page, load time, results
  • test layout of initial page
Not to use it
when where
  • testing completely new experiences
  • long term return data rates
  • if you need to find if something is missing
  • you want to know in which mountain you should be on
  • add premium service
  • online cars selling: will a change increase repeat customers or referrals?
  • update brand, including logo

Examples & ideas

A/B testing ideas

A media company might want to increase readership, increase the amount of time readers spend on their site, and amplify their articles with social sharing. To achieve these goals, they might test variations on:
  • Email sign-up modals
  • Email sign-up modals
  • Social sharing buttons
A travel company may want to increase the number of successful bookings are completed on their website or mobile app, or may want to increase revenue from ancillary purchases. To improve these metrics, they may test variations of:
  • Homepage search modals
  • Search results pages
  • Ancillary product presentation
An e-commerce company might want to increase the number of completed checkouts, the average order value, or increase holiday sales. To accomplish this, they may A/B test:
  • Homepage promotions
  • Navigation elements
  • Checkout funnel components
A technology company might want to increase the number of high-quality leads for their sales team, increase the number of free trial users, or attract a specific type of buyer. They might test:
  • Lead form components
  • Free trial signup flow
  • Homepage messaging and call-to-action

A/B testing examples

Discovery A/B tested the components of their video player to engage with their TV show 'super fan' The result? A 6% increase in video engagement +6%
Sony A/B tested variations of its homepage checkout page layout to increase purchases by 20% +20%
ComScore A/B tested logos and testimonials to increase social proof on a product landing page and increased leads generated by 69% +69%
Secret Escapes tested variations of their mobile signup pages, doubling conversion rates and increasing lifetime value x2

How to protect your participants: main principles

  • goal: highlight types of issues you should keep in mind as you design and run experiments
  • those issues may affect little decisions about authentication and data capture
  • even whether you run the A/B test at all
Risk
  • what risk is the participant being exposed to?
  • main threshold is whether the risk exceeds that of "minimal risk"
  • are participants facing more than minimal risk?
  • if the risk exceeds minimal risk, then informed consent is required
Benefits
  • what benefits might result from the study?
  • the benefits justify the risks?
  • in most online A/B testing, the benefits are around improving the product
  • in other social sciences: understand human condition to help in education and development
  • in medicine: often around improved health outcomes
Alternatives
  • what other choices do participants have?
  • main issue: fewer alternatives out there, the more issue around coercion and whether participants really have a choice in whether to participate or not
  • and how that balances against the risks and benefits
Data sensitivity / privacy
  • what type of data is being collected?, how sensitive is it?
  • what expectation of privacy and confidentiality do participants have?
  • do participants understand what data is being collected about them?
  • data gathered is considered personally identifiable?, is there a high re-identification risk?
  • what harm would befall the individual should that data be made public?
  • how is the data handled?
  • "A Taxonomy of Privacy": classifies things people mean by privacy, to better understand privacy violations

pseudonymous vs anonymous data

Identified
  • data is stored and collected with personally identifiable information
  • HIPAA 18 personally omdetifiers
Pseudonymous
  • data is stored with a randomly generated id
  • such as a cookie that gets assigned on some event
Anonymous
  • data is stored and collected without personally identifiable information
Anonymized
  • identified or anonymous data, guaranteed in some way that the re-identification risk is low to non-existent
