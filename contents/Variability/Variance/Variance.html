Variance

The variance (\(\sigma^2\)-population, \(s^2\)-sample) of a random variable is a measure of spread of a density around its mean. It is is defined as the expected square distance the random variable is from the mean: $$Var\left(X\right) = \sigma^2 = E\left[\left(X-\mu\right)^2\right] = E\left[X^2\right] - E\left[X\right]^2$$

Can be computed as: $$s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=0}^n \left(x_i - \bar{x}\right)^2, \hspace{1.5cm} \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^N \left(x_i - \mu\right)^2$$

The relation for \(s^2\) is shown including the Bessel's correction, and it's related with the degrees of freedom (dof) of the sample

characteristics:

  • densities with higher variance are more spread than those with lower
  • variance is always non-negative
  • small variance = data points tend to be very close to the mean to each other
  • variance of zero indicates that all the values are identical

Bessel's correction

Corrects the bias in the estimation of the population variance from a sample, and some (but not all) of the bias in the estimation of the population standard deviation. To apply Bessel's correction we multiply \(\frac{\sum_{i=0}^n \left(x_i - \bar{x}\right)^2}{n}\) by \(\frac{n}{n-1}\).

Standard deviation

Standard deviation

Standard deviation (\(\sigma\)-population, \(s\)-sample) is the square root of the variance, ad it used to measure distance from the mean. It is the standard way of measuring the variability. $$s = \sqrt{s^2} \hspace{1.5cm} \sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}$$

normal distribution and standard deviation

In normal distributions:

  • 68.26% of the data lies within 1 standard deviation (1s) from the mean,
  • 95.44% within 2 standard deviations (2s), and
  • 99.74% within 3 standard deviation (3s)
