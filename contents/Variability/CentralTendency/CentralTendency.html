Central tendency

Measures of central tendency

  • single value that attempts to describe a set of data
  • by identifying the central position within that set of data

when to use the mean, median and mode


Type of table Best measure of central tendency
Categorical Mode
Ordinal Median
Numerical (not skewed) Mean

Mean

Mean

The mean (\(\mu\)-population, \(\bar{x}\)-sample) of a dataset is the numerical average and can be computed by dividing the sum of all the data points by the number of data points: $$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=0}^{n} x_i, \hspace{1.5cm} \mu = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^{N} x_i$$

The mean is heavily affected by outliers, therefore we say the mean is not a robust measurement.

Unbiased estimator

The mean has the property of being a unbiased estimator, which means that many samples from the same population will have similar means, so the mean of the means will be a unbiased estimator of the population mean.Thus the mean can be used to make inferences about the population it came from.

Median

Median

The median of a dataset is the datapoint that is directly in the middle of the ordered data set. If two numbers are in the middle then the median is the average of the two.

  1. For odd datasets, the position of the middle score is \(\frac{n+1}{2}\), so the $$\text{median} = x_{\frac{n+1}{2}}$$
  2. For even datasets, the position of the two middle scores is \(\frac{n}{2}\) and \(\frac{n}{2} + 1\) , so the $$\text{median} = \frac{x_{\frac{n}{2}} + x_{\frac{n}{2}+1}}{2}$$
The median is robust to outliers, therefore an outlier will not affect the value of the median.

Mode

Mode

The mode of a dataset is the datapoint that occurs the most frequently in the data set.

  • Mode of a distribution: range that occurs with the highest frequency
  • Uniform distribution: no mode
  • Multimodal: two or more distinct clear trends
  • Biased estimator → cannot be used to know a population, only the sample
The mode is robust to outliers as well.
