One-sample tests

One-sample tests

  • determines if the population that sample comes from is significantly different from the population of reference

constructing one-sample hypothesis test

1. State the hypothesis
  • state null and alternative hypotheses
    • \(H_0: \mu = \mu_I\) \(H_1: \mu \neq \mu_I\)
    \(H_0: \mu \geq \mu_I\) \(H_1: \mu < \mu_I\)
    \(H_0: \mu \leq \mu_I\) \(H_1: \mu > \mu_I\)
2. Analyze sample data
  • find the value of the test statistic
  • for example \(\bar{x}\)
3. Calculate test statistic
  • based on whether the standard deviation is known, \(\sigma\), or not, \(s\)
known std, \(\sigma\) unknown std, \(s\)
$$\begin{array}{rcl} \text{z-score} & = & \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{SE} \\ SE & = & \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \end{array}$$ $$\begin{array}{rcl} \text{t-score} & = & \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{SE} \\ SE & = & \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \end{array}$$
4. Determine critical point
  • based on whether the standard deviation is known, \(\sigma\), or not, \(s\)
known std, \(\sigma\) unknown std, \(s\)
$$p^* \rightarrow z^* = CDF^{-1}_{\mathscr{N}(0,1)} \left( 1-\frac{\alpha}{2} \right)$$ $$p^* \rightarrow t^* = CDF^{-1}_{t(\text{dof})} \left( 1-\frac{\alpha}{2} \right)$$
where \(\text{dof} = n-1\) are the degrees of freedom for the Student t distribution
5. Interpret results
  • apply the decision rule for rejecting the null hypothesis
  • if test statistic's value is unlikely → reject the null hypothesis
  • approaches are equivalent
P-value
  • strength of evidence in support of a null hypothesis
  • if P-value \(< \alpha\) → reject null hypothesis
  • probability of observing a test statistic as extreme as calculated from sample, assuming the null hypotheis is true
$$\begin{array}{cc} H_0: & \mu = \mu_I \\ H_1: & \mu \neq \mu_I \end{array}$$\(\) $$\begin{cases} \text{reject } H_0 & \left| \text{p-score} \right| > \left| p^* \right|\\ \text{fail to reject } H_0 & \left| \text{p-score} \right| \leq \left| p^* \right| \end{cases}$$
$$\begin{array}{cc} H_0: & \mu \geq \mu_I\\ H_1: & \mu < \mu_I \end{array}$$ $$\begin{cases} \text{reject } H_0 & \text{p-score} > p^*\\ \text{fail to reject } H_0 & \text{p-score} \leq p^* \end{cases}$$
$$\begin{array}{cc} H_0: & \mu \leq \mu_I\\ H_1: & \mu > \mu_I \end{array}$$ $$\begin{cases} \text{reject } H_0 & \text{p-score} \lt p^*\\ \text{fail to reject } H_0 & \text{p-score} \geq p^* \end{cases}$$
Region of acceptance
  • range of values
  • if test statistic falls in → null hypothesis is not rejected
  • region of rejection: range of values outside the region of acceptance
$$\begin{cases} \text{reject } H_0 & \mu \notin \text{CI}\\ \text{fail to reject } H_0 & \mu \in \text{CI} \end{cases}$$
known std, \(\sigma\) unknown std, \(s\)
$$\begin{array}{rcl} \text{CI} & = & \bar{x} \pm \text{me} \\ \text{me} & = & z^* \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \end{array}$$ $$\begin{array}{rcl} \text{CI} & = & \bar{x} \pm \text{me} \\ \text{me} & = & t^* \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \end{array}$$
where \(n\) is the size of sample used to construct the CI
