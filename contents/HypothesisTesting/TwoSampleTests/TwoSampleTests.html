Two-sample tests

Two-sample tests

  • determines how different two sample means are from each other
  • divided into two types:
    • dependent (within-subjects designs)
    • independent (between-subject designs)

Dependent two-sample test (for paired samples)

  • dependent: when sample subjects takes the test twice
  • deals with within-subjects designs
  • there are three principal cases:
    1. two conditions or treatments → \(H_0 : \mu_1 = \mu_2\)
    2. pre-test + post-test → \(H_0 : \mu_{\text{pre}} = \mu_{\text{post}}\)
    3. longitudinal study: growth over time → \(H_0 : \mu_{\text{time1}} = \mu_{\text{time2}}\)
  • have pair data:
    • \(X_{1i}\) \(X_{2i}\) \(D_i = X_{1i} - X_{2i}\)
    \(X_{11}\) \(X_{12}\) \(X_{11}-X_{21}\)
    \(X_{21}\) \(X_{22}\) \(X_{12}-X_{22}\)
  • advantages & disadvantages of repeated measures studies
    • advantages disadvantages
    • control for individual differences
    • use fewer subjects
    • cost-effective
    • less time-consuming
    • carry-over effects
    • order may influence results

constructing the hypothesis test

  1. Transform the hypothesis to: $$\boxed{\begin{array}{cc} H_{0}: & \mu_{1}=\mu_{2}\\ H_{1}: & \mu_{1}\neq\mu_{2} \end{array}}\Rightarrow\boxed{\begin{array}{cc} H_{0}: & \mu_{1}-\mu_{2}=0\\ H_{1}: & \mu_{1}-\mu_{2}\neq0 \end{array}}\Rightarrow\boxed{\begin{array}{cc} H_{0}: & \mu_{D}=0\\ H_{1}: & \mu_{D}\neq0 \end{array}}$$ where \(\mu_D = \mu_1 - \mu_2\)
  2. Take \(D_i\) as the new one-sample
  3. Proceed with the one-sample test method

construction two-sample hypothesis test

1. State the hypothesis
  • state null and alternative hypotheses
    • \(H_0: \mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0\) \(H_1: \mu_1 - \mu_2 \neq 0\)
    \(H_0: \mu_1 - \mu_2 \leq 0\) \(H_1: \mu_1 - \mu_2 \gt 0\)
    \(H_0: \mu_1 - \mu_2 \geq 0\) \(H_1: \mu_1 - \mu_2 \lt 0\)
2. Analyze sample data
  • find the value of the test statistic
  • for example \(\bar{x}_1\) and \(\bar{x}_2\)
3. Calculate test statistic
  • based on whether the standard deviation is known, \(\sigma\), or not, \(s\)
known std, \(\sigma\) unknown std, \(s\)
$$\begin{array}{rcl} \text{z-score} & = & \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)}{SE} \end{array}$$ $$\begin{array}{rcl} \text{t-score} & = & \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)}{SE} \end{array}$$
\(n1 = n2\)
$$\begin{array}{rcl} \sigma & = & \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2} \\ SE & = & \sqrt{\frac{\sigma_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{\sigma_2^2}{n_2}} \end{array}$$ $$\begin{array}{rcl} s & = & \sqrt{s_1^2 + s_2^2} \\ SE & = & \sqrt{\frac{s_1^2}{n_1} + \frac{s_2^2}{n_2}} \end{array}$$
\(n1 \neq n2\): Pooled variance
$$\begin{array}{rcl} S_p^2 & = & \frac{SS_1 + SS_2}{(n_1 - 1) + (n_2 - 1)} \\ SE & = & \sqrt{\frac{S_p^2}{n_1} + \frac{S_p^2}{n_2}} \end{array}$$ where \(SS_1 = \sum \left( x_i - \bar{x}\right)^2\) → square sum
4. Determine critical point
  • based on whether the standard deviation is known, \(\sigma\), or not, \(s\)
known std, \(\sigma\) unknown std, \(s\)
$$p^* \rightarrow z^* = CDF^{-1}_{\mathscr{N}(0,1)} \left( 1-\frac{\alpha}{2} \right)$$ $$p^* \rightarrow t^* = CDF^{-1}_{t(\text{dof})} \left( 1-\frac{\alpha}{2} \right)$$
where \(\text{dof} = (n_1 -1) + (n_2 -1) \) are the degrees of freedom for the Student t distribution
5. Interpret results
  • apply the decision rule for rejecting the null hypothesis
  • if test statistic's value is unlikely → reject the null hypothesis
  • approaches are equivalent
P-value
  • strength of evidence in support of a null hypothesis
  • if P-value \(< \alpha\) → reject null hypothesis
  • probability of observing a test statistic as extreme as calculated from sample, assuming the null hypotheis is true
$$\begin{array}{cc} H_0: & \mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0 \\ H_1: & \mu_1 - \mu_2 \neq 0 \end{array}$$\(\) $$\begin{cases} \text{reject } H_0 & \left| \text{p-score} \right| > \left| p^* \right|\\ \text{fail to reject } H_0 & \left| \text{p-score} \right| \leq \left| p^* \right| \end{cases}$$
$$\begin{array}{cc} H_0: & \mu_1 - \mu_2 \leq 0\\ H_1: & \mu_1 - \mu_2 \gt 0 \end{array}$$ $$\begin{cases} \text{reject } H_0 & \text{p-score} > p^*\\ \text{fail to reject } H_0 & \text{p-score} \leq p^* \end{cases}$$
$$\begin{array}{cc} H_0: & \mu_1 - \mu_2 \geq 0\\ H_1: & \mu_1 - \mu_2 \lt 0 \end{array}$$ $$\begin{cases} \text{reject } H_0 & \text{p-score} \lt p^*\\ \text{fail to reject } H_0 & \text{p-score} \geq p^* \end{cases}$$
Region of acceptance
  • range of values
  • if test statistic falls in → null hypothesis is not rejected
  • region of rejection: range of values outside the region of acceptance
$$\begin{cases} \text{reject } H_0 & \mu \notin \text{CI}\\ \text{fail to reject } H_0 & \mu \in \text{CI} \end{cases}$$
known std, \(\sigma\) unknown std, \(s\)
$$\begin{array}{rcl} \text{CI} & = & (\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2) \pm \text{me} \\ \text{me} & = & z^* \cdot SE \end{array}$$ $$\begin{array}{rcl} \text{CI} & = & (\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2) \pm \text{me} \\ \text{me} & = & t^* \cdot SE \end{array}$$
where \(n\) is the size of sample used to construct the CI

T-test assumptions

  • \(x\) and \(y\) should be random samples from two independent populations.
  • Populations are approximately normal (less important when \(n>30\)
  • Sample data can estimate population variances
  • Population variances are roughly equal (allowing s to use the pooled variance)
