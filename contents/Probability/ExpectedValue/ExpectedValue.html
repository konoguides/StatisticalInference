Expected value

Expected value

  • denoted as \(E[X]\), and is a property of distributions
  • the expected value or mean of a random variable is the center of mass of its distribution
  • is a measyre of its central tendency
  • intuitively: the long-run avarage value of repetitions of the experiment
  • for dicrete random variables: probability-weighted aaverage of all possible values $$E[X] = \sum_{x}^{X} x \cdot P(x)$$
  • for continuos random variables: sum is replaced by integral, and PMF by PDF $$E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x\cdot PDF(x) dx$$
  • doesn't exist for random variables having distributions with large "tails"; e.g., Cuchy

Constants

The expected value of a constant is equal to the constant itself, i.e, if \(c\) is a constant, then: $$E[c] = c$$

Monotonacity

If \(X\) and \(Y\) are random variables such that \(X \leq Y\) almost surely, then: $$E[X] \leq E[Y]$$

Linearity

The expected value operator \(E\) is linear in the sense that: $$\begin{array}{rll} E[X + c] & = & E[X] + c \\ E[X + Y] & = & E[X] + E[Y] \\ E[aX] & = & aE[X] \end{array}$$

Population mean vs sample mean

  • population mean is the center of mass of population
  • sample mean is the center of mass of the observed data
  • sample mean is an estimator of population mean
  • more data into sample mean, more concentrated its PDF / PMF around population mean
  • sample mean is an unbiased estimator
    • the mean of its distribution is the mean trying to estimate
